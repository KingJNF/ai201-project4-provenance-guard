# Provenance Guard — Planning Document

A backend system that classifies whether submitted text-based creative work was written by a human or generated by AI, scores confidence in that classification, surfaces a plain-language transparency label, and handles creator appeals.

---

## 1. Detection Signals

Provenance Guard uses **two independent signals** that capture genuinely different properties of the text: one *semantic*, one *structural*.

### Signal 1 — LLM Classifier (Groq, `llama-3.3-70b-versatile`)
- **Measures:** Holistic semantic and stylistic coherence — naturalness of voice, specificity of lived detail, originality of phrasing, presence of generic "AI-sounding" transitions, and over-smoothness.
- **Why it differs human vs. AI:** AI prose trends fluent, hedged, and evenly paced with generic connectives ("It is important to note," "Furthermore").  Humans inject idiosyncrasy, concrete detail, and tonal irregularity.
- **Output:** A float `ai_likelihood` in `[0.0, 1.0]` (0 = certainly human, 1 = certainly AI) plus a one-sentence rationale.
- **Blind spot:** Fooled by lightly-edited AI output and by formal/polished human writing (academic, technical) that shares surface features with AI. Non-deterministic at the margins.

### Signal 2 — Stylometric Analyzer (pure Python)
- **Measures:** Quantifiable structural variability via three sub-metrics:
  - **Sentence-length variance** — do sentence lengths fluctuate or stay uniform?
  - **Type-token ratio (TTR)** — vocabulary diversity (unique words ÷ total words).
  - **Punctuation density** — rate and variety of punctuation usage.
- **Why it differs human vs. AI:** AI writing tends to be statistically *uniform*; human writing is more *variable* (a short sentence beside a long one, unexpected word choices, erratic punctuation).
- **Output:** A float `stylometric_score` in `[0.0, 1.0]`, where higher uniformity → more AI-like.
- **Blind spot:** Blind to meaning entirely. Formal human registers (legal, academic) score AI-like; AI prompted to "write casually" scores human-like. Unreliable on short texts (too few sentences for variance to be meaningful).

### Why these two
Their blind spots are *different*. Formal human prose fools the stylometric signal, but the LLM may catch a genuine voice; casually-prompted AI fools stylometrics, but the LLM may catch the semantic emptiness. Combining a semantic judge with a structural measurer covers more ground than either alone.

### How they combine
Both signals output an **AI-likelihood in `[0,1]`**. The combined score is a **weighted average favoring the LLM signal**, because the LLM is the more informative judge across the widest range of inputs, while stylometrics is a secondary, structural sanity-check:

combined_ai_likelihood = (0.65 × llm_score) + (0.35 × stylometric_score)
   
Weighting and rationale are revisited under the Ensemble stretch feature.

## 2. Uncertainty Representation

### What a score means to the system
The combined value is an **AI-likelihood**: how strongly the evidence points to AI authorship. We convert it into the user-facing **confidence** of whichever verdict we give (so a non-technical reader never sees "0.62 AI". They see a confidence in the stated result).

### The thresholds (asymmetric by design)
A false positive labeling a human's work as AI is **worse** than the reverse on a writing platform. So the bar to declare `likely_ai` is set *higher* than the bar to clear someone as `likely_human`, and the `uncertain` band is deliberately wide:

| Combined AI-likelihood | Attribution      | User-facing confidence            |
|------------------------|------------------|-----------------------------------|
| `>= 0.75`              | `likely_ai`      | `combined_score`                  |
| `0.40 – 0.749`         | `uncertain`      | `1 - (2 × |score - 0.5|)` *(peaks at 0.5)* |
| `< 0.40`               | `likely_human`   | `1 - combined_score`              |

- It takes **0.75+** evidence to accuse (high bar), but only dropping **below 0.40** to clear as human (lower bar) — the asymmetry protects creators.
- A `0.51` lands in **uncertain**; a `0.95` lands in **likely_ai**. These produce *meaningfully different labels*, not a binary flip at 0.5.

### What 0.5 means to a user
0.5 is the center of the **uncertain** band — the system saying "I genuinely cannot tell." The label at that point is explicitly inconclusive and never accuses.

### Validation plan
Test 4 deliberately chosen inputs (clear AI, clear human, formal human, lightly-edited AI). Confirm the clear cases land in `likely_ai` / `likely_human` with high confidence and the borderline cases land in `uncertain`. If a score defies intuition, print both signal scores separately to find the misbehaving one.

## 3. Transparency Label Design

Three variants, plain language, no jargon. Each shows a verdict line, a confidence line, and a context line. (Exact strings below are canonical.)

**High-confidence AI** (`likely_ai`, e.g. confidence 0.88):
> 🤖 **Likely AI-generated.**
> Our analysis suggests this text was probably created with the help of an AI tool (confidence: high, 88%). This is an automated estimate, not a certainty. The creator can request a review if they disagree.

**High-confidence human** (`likely_human`, e.g. confidence 0.91):
> ✍️ **Likely human-written.**
> Our analysis suggests this text was probably written by a person (confidence: high, 91%). This is an automated estimate, not a guarantee.

**Uncertain** (`uncertain`, e.g. confidence 0.50):
> ❓ **Origin unclear.**
> We couldn't confidently determine whether this text was written by a person or generated by AI (confidence: low). Please treat the origin as unknown. Human authorship is entirely plausible.

Confidence is also bucketed into plain words for readers:
`>= 0.80` → "high", `0.60–0.79` → "moderate", `< 0.60` → "low".

## 4. Appeals Workflow

- **Who can appeal:** The creator of any classified submission.
- **What they provide:** The `content_id` and free-text `creator_reasoning` explaining why they believe the classification is wrong.
- **What the system does on receipt:**
  1. Looks up the original classification by `content_id`.
  2. Updates that content's status to **`under_review`**.
  3. Writes an **appeal event** to the audit log, capturing the reasoning and referencing the original decision (so both sit side-by-side in the log).
  4. Returns a confirmation that the appeal was received. *(No automated re-classification. A human reviewer handles it.)*
- **What a reviewer sees in the queue:** For each appeal — original attribution, combined confidence, both individual signal scores, timestamps, and the creator's stated reasoning with everything needed to overturn or uphold.

---

## 5. Anticipated Edge Cases

1. **Non-native English speaker writing a formal personal essay.** Their formal, even register makes the *stylometric* signal read uniform → AI-like, and the LLM may lean mildly AI on polished prose. Both signals' blind spots align here, so this risks a false positive. **Mitigation:** the wide `uncertain` band and high `likely_ai` bar route this to "Origin unclear" rather than an accusation, and the appeals path gives the creator recourse.

2. **A short, repetitive minimalist poem with simple vocabulary.** Low type-token ratio and uniform short lines make the *stylometric* signal score it AI-like, while there's too little text for sentence-variance to be meaningful. The LLM may also struggle to find "voice" in so few words.
**Mitigation:** short-text inputs are flagged as low-reliability and pushed toward `uncertain` rather than a confident verdict.

In the appeal flow, a creator submits a `content_id` and reasoning to `POST /appeal`; the system flips the content's status to `under_review`, logs the appeal beside the original decision, and returns a confirmation.

## API Surface

| Method & Route | Accepts | Returns |
|---|---|---|
| `POST /submit` | `{ text, creator_id, content_type? (default "text") }` | `{ content_id, attribution, confidence, label, signals:{llm_score, stylometric_score}, status }` |
| `POST /appeal` | `{ content_id, creator_reasoning }` | `{ content_id, status:"under_review", message }` |
| `GET /log` | — | `{ entries: [ …structured audit entries… ] }` |
| `GET /health` | — | `{ status:"ok" }` |

`content_type` is a dispatcher seam (default `"text"`) so the Ensemble and Multi-Modal stretch features can be added without changing the core contract.

## Stretch Feature Plan (update before starting each)

### Ensemble Detection (implemented)
Third signal: **Repetition/burstiness heuristic** measures repeated bigrams and repeated sentence-opening words. AI text tends to reuse phrasing and openers; human writing varies more. Output: 0–1 ai_likelihood (higher = more
repetitive = more AI-like).

**Weighting/voting strategy:** Weighted average — LLM 0.70, stylometric 0.15, repetition 0.15. The LLM dominates as the proven-reliable semantic judge.
**Conflict resolution:** The repetition signal uses a *neutral baseline* (0.5): absence of repetition is treated as "no evidence," not as a vote for human, so it only influences the verdict when repetition is actually detected. This
prevents a non-discriminating structural signal from diluting a strong semantic judgment which is a deliberate abstention design.


- **Analytics Dashboard:** a read-only view over the audit log — AI-vs-human verdict ratio, appeal rate, and average confidence.
- **Provenance Certificate:** a "verified human" credential earned via an extra verification step, displayed as a distinct label variant.
- **Multi-Modal Support:** add a `content_type:"metadata"` branch in the dispatcher with its own signal (text stylometrics don't apply to metadata).
